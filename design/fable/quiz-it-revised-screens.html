QUIZ-IT · CRITICAL SCREEN REVIEW

Revised high-fidelity mock-ups for the eight flagged screens. Reviewed from two seats: the host mid-performance, and a player three drinks in at the back table. The established design language is untouched — every change is either information the room was missing, or drama the moment was owed. Each mock-up is followed by the change log and the reasoning.

1 · DISPLAY LOBBY — revised

Agreed: the old lobby wasted the highest-dwell screen time of the night. An empty premium screen isn't premium, it's closed. The revision makes the lobby a recruitment engine: the PIN becomes the room's biggest object, and the right half pays off the join instantly — your crest, on television, seconds after you enter.

What changed and why:
  • The PIN is now the largest object on screen — statement-XL numerals with the brand bloom behind it. A lobby has exactly one job and the old screen whispered it. Host's seat: "I can now say 'the PIN's on the screen' and stop spelling out URLs."
  • The crest wall fills the right half. Every join lands with a flare (see "Late Entry"). This is the recruitment loop: a table joins → the room sees it → two more tables reach for their phones. Social proof was the useful content the empty screen owed us.
  • Three-step how-to sits with the QR — support-size, glanceable, never competing with the PIN.
  • Footer works too: live countdown (commitment device) and the format tease (names that provoke table talk: "what's the Hard Deck?"). No countdown before T-10, per the Pre-Show spec.
  • What was deliberately NOT added: rules text, sponsor clutter, music info. The lobby recruits; it doesn't lecture.

2 · PLAYER JOIN — PIN entry, revised

The old join was the one moment Quiz-It felt like a website — a text field and an OS keyboard. The revision builds a custom keypad styled as show hardware: four glowing slots, big keys, auto-advance. Entering a PIN should feel like arming something.

What changed and why:
  • Custom keypad, not the OS keyboard. Big show-hardware keys in the thumb zone, pressed-state weight, a Tick per digit. The OS keyboard is the single fastest way to feel like a form.
  • Four slots are the hero — filled slots seal with an edge-glow, the next slot pulses (the screen tells you where you are without reading). Auto-submit on the fourth digit: no "Join" button, no confirm step. Four taps and you're in.
  • Wrong PIN is dignified: slots empty with a soft dim and one line — "That PIN isn't live — check the big screen." No red flash, no shake (red means incorrect answers; shakes are panic).
  • One whisper of wit ("It's the big number on the screen") because this is a light moment — and it pre-answers the only question people actually have.

What changed and why:
  • The fan. Three cards held like a hand of cards — rotated, overlapping, the focused card raised. Card faces are art-directed objects: deep feature-colour gradients, engraved caps, inner frame line, and a slow foil sheen that makes them feel printed on expensive stock.   • Hold-to-play with a charging fill replaces tap-to-use. Spending a card is a ritual, and the charge prevents drink-fumble accidents. The line "The room will know" sells the payoff: your private gesture becomes public theatre on the Display.

  • Answer screen: selection now visibly costs the others (non-selected options drop to 42%), the LOCK bar appears only after selection, and it carries the draining speed-bonus shimmer with one honest whisper line. Selection is instinct; locking is commitment — the thumb feels the difference.

4 · QUESTION DISPLAY — live answer meter, revised

Agreed: a progress bar that isn't data is wallpaper. The old bar animated on a timer regardless of reality. The revision replaces it with the lock meter: one segment per team, lighting the instant that team locks — real information, host ammunition, and social pressure in one strip.

What changed and why:
  • One segment per team, lit in lock order. The meter now answers a real question — "how many are in?" — and creates the room's best mid-question pressure: watching the strip fill while your table argues. Segments are anonymous (no crests) so nobody's slowness is shamed; the count is public, the culprits aren't.
  • The count is host material: "NINE of fourteen in — five tables still fighting about it!" The old decorative bar gave the host nothing to say.
  • Time stays on the ring (screen edge, peripheral) — the meter is answers, the ring is time, and they never share a visual. Two data channels, zero ambiguity, one-second read.
  • Newest lock lands with a scale-tick — the strip visibly ticks like a turnstile; motion only on state change.

5 · LEADERBOARD — gap bars replace coloured bars

Agreed — and the coloured bars had to go for two reasons: per-team colours violate the locked semantic palette (colour = meaning, and team-colour meant nothing), and bar-length-as-points is unreadable at a glance when scores are close. Replaced with gap bars: every bar shows score relative to the leader, in brand purple. Now bar length answers the only question the room asks: how far off the front are we?

What changed and why:
  • Two colossal cards dominate the stage: the known card (ivory stock, classic pips — the one legitimately non-dark surface in the show, which is why it commands the eye) and the unknown card, crest-backed, purple-lit, holding its secret. Everything in the format is these two objects; now the screen says so.

  • One caption line for the room ("…are deciding…") replaces all instructional text. If the format needs explaining on screen, the screen has failed; ladder + card + two words explain it in one look.

7 · THE PURSUIT — finish prominence & runner visibility

Fair challenge on both counts. The finish column read as furniture, and at small tiers runners could sink into the stage. Fixes: the finish becomes a breathing beacon (brighter chequer, stronger edge, slow pulse — the only ambient glow on the board, so the eye is pulled rightward all round), and runners gain a dark under-shadow plus a raised brightness floor so they pop at every tier.

What changed and why (per tier):
  • The finish beacon. Chequer contrast doubled, white edge strengthened, and a slow 3s glow-pulse — the board's only ambient light, making it the gravitational pull of the whole layout. When the sweep fires at gate 7, it's a climax of something the eye already knows.
  • Runner visibility floor: every runner now carries a dark drop-shadow beneath its glow (separation from green blocks behind it) and a minimum brightness regardless of lane state. At PELOTON (40 teams) the runner may never render below 24px and trails shorten rather than the figure shrinking further.
  • Tier review verdicts: 2 teams — GRAND holds, but lanes cap at 1.35× height so the board never looks like two lonely strips (verified in the prototype); 8 — FIELD is the showcase, unchanged; 16/24 — DIVISION's mirrored columns keep one shared central finish: both halves race toward the beacon; 40 — PELOTON survives only because of the visibility floor + compaction; names drop to whisper weight but crests never shrink below 18px.
  • Banked tally stays bright on dead lanes — elimination reads as "holding 20," not "deleted."

8 · WINNER CEREMONY — the coronation, revised

Agreed. If this isn't the biggest visual moment of the night, the night has no summit. The revision commits fully: the room's only gold flood, rising light rays, real confetti weather, the crowned crest, the name at maximum scale — and the photo ritual built into the screen. Composed so a vertical phone crop of the centre is a perfect nine-second story clip.

What changed and why:
  • Total gold commitment. The floor-up gold flood + slow-turning light rays repaint the entire venue for the only time all night. Because gold has appeared nowhere since doors opened, this single screen is worth two hours of restraint — the payoff of the whole colour constitution.
  • Vertical-crop composition: kicker → crowned crest → name → CHAMPIONS stack dead-centre in a 9:16 window. A hundred phones film this; the frame is designed for their cameras, not just the room.
  • Confetti as weather, with an end: storm → drift → done within ten seconds (the mock loops for demonstration only). Then the screen holds its composed gold state — still — for photos; nothing pulses while cameras are out.
  • The photo ritual is on the screen itself ("Get up here — your photo's waiting"), steering the winners into the champion-backdrop moment that becomes their share card. The ceremony now produces its own marketing artefact.
  • The name outranks the word CHAMPIONS. The team is the hero of this frame; the show signs it quietly in the corner, as always.
All eight revisions stay inside the locked system: no new colours, no new type sizes, no glass in the show layer, motion only on state change, metals and confetti reserved for the finale. Reference: Experience Bible · Design System · Motion Language · Brand Voice · Constitution.
