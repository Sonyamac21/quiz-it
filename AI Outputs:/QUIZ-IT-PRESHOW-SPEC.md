# PART A — THE DOCTRINE OF THE WARM-UP

Television learned this a century after theatre did: **the show starts before the show starts.** The pre-show's job is to convert walk-ins into an audience *before* the Opening Titles ask them to be one — so that when the blackout hits, the room is already leaning forward.

The pre-show has one enemy: the feeling of *waiting*. Nothing below is a waiting screen. Every minute is designed to do one of three jobs: **recruit** (get teams joined), **warm** (raise social energy), or **promise** (signal that something premium is coming). A minute doing none of these is a defect.

---

# PART B — THE TIMELINE

The pre-show runs in five phases keyed to show time (T-0 = Opening Titles). All timings compress gracefully for shorter lead-ins; the *order* never changes.

## Phase 1 — VENUE MODE (doors → T-30) · "Something's on tonight"

**Display:** The **idle loop** (Part C) runs at its calmest gear: wordmark breathing on the dark stage, tonight's show name and start time, the join instruction rotating gently. No countdown yet — a countdown running for two hours is wallpaper.
**Music:** The venue's own. Quiz-It stays out of the air entirely.
**Handset:** Anyone who joins early gets the full lobby experience (crest birth, "Waiting for your host"), then the phone rests — early joiners must never feel they joined too early.
**Host:** Not yet visible as a performer. Walking tables informally: "You in tonight? Grab the table card."
**Audience psychology:** At this range the screen is *décor that promises*. The purple glow in the room does the same job as a stage with instruments on it — people who've never heard of Quiz-It register that this venue has *entertainment infrastructure*. Emotional objective: **curiosity without obligation.**

## Phase 2 — THE GATHERING (T-30 → T-10) · "People are in. Are you?"

**Display:** The idle loop shifts gears: the **crest wall** becomes the hero. Every team that joins appears on the big screen within seconds of joining — crest assembling, name beneath, taking its place in a growing constellation. The join instruction sharpens: "14 teams in. Join at your table."
**Music:** Quiz-It's pre-show bed enters under licence of the venue — warm, mid-tempo, confident (Audio Guide, Part F) — lifting perceptibly at T-20 and T-15.
**Handset:** Full joining flow live (Part D). Lobby now shows the same team count the room sees.
**Host:** First mic moment at ~T-20 (Part F script): a welcome, not an instruction manual.
**Audience psychology:** This phase runs on **social proof**. A name appearing on the big screen is the pre-show's core loop: a table joins → the room sees it → two more tables reach for the card. The crest wall converts joining from admin into *a public act with a reward*. Emotional objective: **belonging, and mild fear of being left out.**

## Phase 3 — THE COUNTDOWN (T-10 → T-2) · "It's really happening"

**Display:** The countdown appears for the first time — a dignified clock, not a bomb timer: "SHOW STARTS · 08:42," sharing the stage with the crest wall. At T-5 the stage darkens one step and the clock takes the bloom. Join messaging shifts to urgency-in-the-show's-voice: "Doors closing on Round 1 — join now to play from the start."
**Music:** The bed steps up at T-10 and again at T-5 — brighter, more percussive, unmistakably *going somewhere*.
**Handset:** Joined phones give a single Pulse at T-5 with a lobby line: "Five minutes. Settle in." (The phone's first proof it knows what time it is.)
**Host:** The T-10 and T-5 announcements (Part F) — recruiting stragglers, seeding the format ("three rounds, a slot machine, and a card game that's ended friendships").
**Audience psychology:** The countdown's late arrival is deliberate: scarcity of the countdown makes the countdown mean something. Ten minutes is short enough to feel real, long enough to get a drink in before it starts — which the host says out loud, because a room with fresh drinks at T-0 stays seated for Round 1. Emotional objective: **anticipation with a deadline.**

## Phase 4 — THE LAST TWO MINUTES (T-2 → T-0) · the held breath

**Display:** The crest wall performs its **final assembly**: all crests drift slowly toward centre, tightening around the clock as it counts under 2:00. At T-0:30 the join messaging disappears entirely (joining continues silently — see Part E — but the *screen* stops recruiting; the show is now inevitable). The last ten seconds are counted by the clock alone — no numerals-stamp, no heartbeat. Those belong to the Opening Titles and the Close; spending them here would counterfeit the show's own currency.
**Music:** The bed thins deliberately — energy converting to tension — and cuts to silence at T-0:10.
**Handset:** At T-0:30, every joined phone eases to a dark, quiet lobby: crest, "It's time.", nothing else.
**Host:** In position, mic up, final line landed by T-0:15 ("Phones out, eyes up — here we go").
**Audience psychology:** The room has been rising for half an hour; the last thirty seconds *withhold*. Silence and a dimming screen make sixty conversations die on their own. The blackout that opens the Ignition then lands on a room already holding its breath. Emotional objective: **the intake of breath before the first note.**

## Phase 5 — T-0 · IGNITION

The Opening Titles storyboard fires exactly as scripted (blackout → heartbeat → roll-call → wordmark). Note the dovetail: the roll-call re-uses the crests the room has watched accumulate all evening — the pre-show built the cast list, the titles introduce them. Nothing in the pre-show may visually outgun the Ignition; the pre-show's ceiling is Gear 1.

# PART C — THE DISPLAY IDLE LOOP

A broadcast holding pattern, not a screensaver. One slow scene rotation (each scene 20–40s, crossfaded gently, full cycle ~3 minutes so a customer looking twice never sees a frozen screen):

1. **The marque** — wordmark breathing, show name, start time.
2. **The invitation** — join instruction with table-card illustration and QR (Part D), one line: "Play tonight. Your phone is the controller."
3. **The crest wall** — teams so far (from Phase 2 onward this scene dominates the rotation).
4. **The format tease** — one card per rotation, Whisper-styled: "3 rounds · Spin to Win · The Hard Deck." Never rules — only names that provoke questions.
5. **The venue/sponsor slot** — see below.

**Sponsor rotations:** sponsor and venue messages ride the idle loop only — never the show. One sponsor card per rotation maximum, rendered in the show's own typography and stage palette (the show lends its voice; it never wears someone else's). Sponsor content is subject to the same content-safety rule as everything else, and sponsor cards vanish permanently at T-10 — the last ten minutes belong to the show. This placement is honest to venues too: the pre-show loop is the *highest-dwell* screen time of the night.

**Idle loop rules:** no countdown before T-10; no audio of its own before Phase 2; max one scene with motion at a time; everything battery-cheap and burn-in-safe (slow positional drift on static elements).

# PART D — JOINING: NFC FIRST, QR ALWAYS

**The table card** is the physical ambassador: a premium printed card (stage-dark, purple edge, crest-embossed) standing on every table. Its copy is an invitation, not instructions: **"TAP TO PLAY"** above the NFC zone; a QR and short URL beneath as the visible fallback ("or scan").

**NFC onboarding (the magic path):** phone taps card → join screen opens directly. Tap-to-playing in under fifteen seconds, no typing a URL, no app. The tap itself is theatre — one guest taps, the table watches it *just work*, and the card gets passed round. Design rule: the NFC join lands on exactly the same join screen as every other path — one welcome, regardless of door.

**QR fallback:** for phones without NFC, cautious guests ("QR feels safer than tapping"), and the Display itself (the invitation scene carries the QR large enough to scan from mid-room). The short URL is the fallback's fallback, and the host can read it aloud.

**Handset behaviour on join:** exactly as the Handset Spec's B1–B2 — "Join the show" → "Who are you tonight?" → crest birth → lobby. The pre-show adds one grace note: teams joining in Phase 2+ see their crest appear on the big screen within seconds of naming themselves, and the phone tells them to look: "You're on the wall. Eyes up."

# PART E — LATE JOINS

**The rule: the door never closes; the ceremony does.** Joining stays open all night — a table arriving at Round 2 is revenue and next week's regulars — but late joins are folded in without disrupting the show:

- After T-0, joining is silent: no crest-wall moment, no announcements. The new team's crest simply exists in the next natural leaderboard build (zero-point rows appear immediately — identity from the first second, per the locked scoring behaviour).
- The late handset gets the compressed welcome (Handset C2): "You're in — Round 2, Question 4 is live," dealt straight into the current state. Never an apology, never "you missed…" — the phone says what's *now*, not what's gone.
- The host console surfaces a quiet toast ("New team: The Latecomers") so the host can choose to welcome them aloud — a host decision, never an automatic interruption.
- Fairness is presentation, not scoring: late teams compete from where they land; the final leaderboard's "biggest climber" language gives them their story if they surge.

# PART F — THE HOST SCRIPT (BEATS, NOT LINES)

Hosts perform in their own voice; Quiz-It scripts *beats* — each with its moment, its job, and a sample line the host may steal:

| When | Beat | Job | Sample line |
|---|---|---|---|
| ~T-20 | **The welcome** | Exist as a performer; point at the table cards | "Evening, all. If there's a purple card on your table, you're sitting in a game show." |
| ~T-10 | **The recruit** | Convert watchers; seed the format | "Ten minutes. Tap the card, name your team. Three rounds, a slot machine, and a card game that's ended friendships." |
| ~T-5 | **The drinks call** | Fill glasses before lock-in; keep energy up | "Five minutes — get the round in now, you won't want the bar during the Hard Deck." |
| ~T-2 | **The seating** | Physically settle the room | "Two minutes. Find your seats, captains hold the phones." |
| T-0:15 | **The arm** | Final hush; hand the room to the screen | "Phones out. Eyes up. Here we go." |

Announcement rules: never more than one mic moment per phase (a host who talks all pre-show has nothing left to spend); every beat names a *time* and an *action*; the T-0:15 line is sacred — short, rhythmic, and the same every week, because ritual lines become the show's liturgy (regulars will say it along).

# PART G — THE EMOTIONAL TIMELINE, MINUTE BY MINUTE

The final fifteen, as an emotional score (earlier minutes hold Phase 1–2 objectives):

| Minute | The room should feel |
|---|---|
| T-15 | "This place has something on tonight." (curiosity) |
| T-14 → T-11 | "People are joining. That's us on the screen!" (belonging, social proof) |
| T-10 | "It's real — there's a clock." (commitment point) |
| T-9 → T-6 | "We need a team name. Something brilliant." (creative play — naming is the first game) |
| T-5 | "Drinks in, we're set." (readiness; the phone pulsed — it *knows*) |
| T-4 → T-3 | "What's the Hard Deck?" (format intrigue, tables trading theories) |
| T-2 | "It's tightening." (the crests converge; the room notices the screen changing) |
| T-1 | "Shh — it's starting." (self-policing quiet begins) |
| T-0:30 | Stillness spreading table to table (the screen stopped recruiting; the music thinned) |
| T-0:10 | Silence. Dark. (the held breath) |
| T-0 | Blackout. The heartbeat. The show. |

The pre-show succeeds when the Opening Titles' blackout is not an interruption but a *release* — the moment the room was promised for thirty minutes, arriving exactly on time.
