# PART A — HOW THE SHOW WORKS AS A SHOW

Before the ten moments, four production principles that govern all of them.

## A1. The Display is the venue's lighting rig

Most venues give us no control over house lights. But a large bright screen in a dim room **is** the lighting rig — when the Display floods purple, the room turns purple; when it blacks out, the room visibly darkens. Every moment below is designed twice: as an image on screen, and as **light thrown onto faces**. Reveals aren't just seen; they change the colour of the room. This is how a TV screen becomes a stage without a single extra fixture.

## A2. The room is the instrument

Quiz-It has something no television show has ever had: **every audience member is holding a synchronised haptic device.** The Handset spec established the vocabulary (Pulse, Hit, Cascade, the Eyes Up state); this document plays it as an orchestra. When sixty phones beat in time with the countdown, the audience isn't watching the show — they're *inside* it. Synchronised room-wide haptics are Quiz-It's invisible signature: competitors can copy a screen animation over a weekend; they cannot copy a room that beats.

## A3. The tension budget — the night has gears

The Bible demands rationed spectacle. Here is the ration, as a rundown card:

| Gear | Used for | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Play** | Questions, standard closes, standard reveals | Crisp, fast, confident |
| **2 — Event** | Leaderboards, round finishes, streak callouts | Heightened, celebratory |
| **3 — Set-piece** | Spin to Win, The Hard Deck, final-question close | Cinematic, room-stopping |
| **4 — Finale** | Podium, Champion Celebration | Everything we have. Once a night. |

The gears only climb through the night. A Gear-3 treatment on Question 2 bankrupts the finale; the discipline of Gear 1 for ninety minutes is what makes Gear 4 detonate.

## A4. The host owns every beat

No moment below auto-advances. Every reveal, every pause, every release fires on the host's trigger. The Display creates *loaded moments*; the host decides when to pull the trigger — that is what makes the host look like the star of a production rather than the operator of a screen. Where a moment has a "hold," the hold lasts as long as the host wants. The software waits; the room never waits for the software.

# PART B — THE TEN MOMENTS

---

## MOMENT 1 — OPENING TITLES
### "The Ignition"

**Emotional objective:** Convert a room of diners into an audience in thirty seconds. Curiosity → attention → collective anticipation. The person mid-conversation must *choose* to stop talking.

**The sequence (host-triggered, ~30 seconds):**

1. **The blackout (0–3s).** Whatever ambient state the screen held — gone. The Display drops to true dark. In a venue, a big screen going black is louder than any graphic: light leaves the room and heads turn toward the absence. Silence from the system.
2. **The heartbeat (3–10s).** A single purple bloom begins to pulse at screen centre, slow and deep, with a low sub-bass pulse under it — felt in the floor more than heard. Every joined phone in the room Pulses *in time with it*. People feel their phones beating with the screen before a single word appears. Conversations fall away on their own.
3. **The roll-call (10–22s).** Team crests — every team in the room — sweep across the stage in a fast, elegant procession, each catching an edge-light as it passes, each team's phone giving a private Tick as *their* crest crosses the big screen. Sixty people just saw themselves on television. Tables point. This is the moment the audience realises the show knows they exist.
4. **The ignition (22–30s).** The procession converges into the bloom — beat of black — then the **QUIZ-IT wordmark detonates on** (Bruno Ace SC, "QUIZ-" purple, "IT" white) with the full sonic logo, light flooding the room purple. Under it, one line: tonight's venue name. The host walks into that light with the microphone.

**Host experience:** The dashboard shows a simple armed sequence — the host triggers the blackout when *they* are ready to open, and the titles land them at their first line. The show makes their entrance.

**Handsets:** Synchronised Pulse through the heartbeat, private Tick at their crest, then the B3 "You're in" handshake as Round 1 begins.

**Why it becomes memorable:** The roll-call. Every opening-title sequence in television is about the show; ours is about *the room*. "Our name came up on the screen and our phones buzzed" is the first story of the night — and it's told before Question 1.

## MOMENT 2 — QUESTION REVEAL
### "The Deal"

**Emotional objective:** A clean gear-shift from social noise to focus. Attention must travel host → screen in under a second, sixty times a night, without ever wearing out.

**What the audience sees:** The stage darkens one step (the room dims with it — the attention cue is *light*, not noise). A card back — large, crest-of-the-show embossed, edge-lit — deals onto centre stage with a single crisp snap-and-settle. The category and question number sit on its face. The room now knows a question exists; the host reads their patter off the tension. On the host's trigger, the card **flips once** — fast, confident, no 3D indulgence — and the question stands at Statement scale, three lines maximum, alone on the stage. Options (when multiple choice) land a beat later in a fast stagger beneath it, and the timer ring ignites around the screen's edge as play opens.

**Audio:** One short dealt-card sting for the arrival; one clean flip note for the reveal; then the low tension bed enters under open play — ducked to nothing whenever the host speaks.

**Host experience:** Two triggers — *deal* and *flip* — so the host controls the tease ("this one's worth double… ready?"). The dashboard shows the question and answer privately from the deal, so the host is never reading cold.

**Handsets:** The deal-in Pulse (Handset B4) lands with the on-screen deal — the shoulder-tap that says *pick up your phone* — then the phone flips to the live question with the stage.

**Pacing:** Deal to flip: entirely the host's. Flip to playable: under two seconds. Sixty times a night, this must feel like a dealer who has done it forever — the premium is in the *sameness*, the ritual reliability of it.

**Why it becomes memorable:** Not as one moment but as a rhythm — the deal becomes the show's heartbeat, and by Round 2 the room responds to the light-drop like a bell. Ritual is what makes a format feel like a *format*.

## MOMENT 3 — FINAL FIVE SECONDS
### "The Close" — Quiz-It's countdown

**Emotional objective:** Manufacture five seconds of pure, repeatable adrenaline — recognisable enough that regulars perform it.

**The design — a countdown the room does:**

At five seconds, the show changes state: the tension bed cuts dead. Into the silence, the **Quiz-It heartbeat** — the same deep dual-thump from the Opening Titles, once per second. On each thump, three things happen at exactly the same instant: the giant numeral (5… 4… 3…) **stamps** onto the stage with a light-shock that flares the whole room, the timer ring contracts one visible step inward, and **every un-locked phone in the venue strikes a Pulse in the hand.** Screen, room-light, and sixty phones, beating as one machine. The stage visibly darkens step by step as the ring closes — the walls closing in, urgency by contraction, not by red (red is spent on nothing but incorrectness, ever).

At zero: no klaxon, no explosion. **"TIME'S UP"** stamps once, the ring seals shut with a heavy vault note, every phone strikes one final Hit — and dead silence. That sudden silence is the loudest thing in the sequence; it hands the room, breath held, straight to the host.

**A challenge to my own first idea:** the obvious design is sirens, shaking, red flashes — game-show clichés that read as panic and wear out by week two. The Close is built instead on *contraction and unison*: the room squeezing shut in perfect sync. Quieter, and far more addictive.

**The escalation rule (tension budget):** Standard questions run the Close at Gear 1 — thump, stamp, contract, done. The *final question of the night* runs it at Gear 3: heartbeat slower and deeper, numerals larger, the darkness closing further, the host cued to milk it. Same language, higher gear — regulars will feel the difference in their chest.

**Host experience:** The dashboard shows the same countdown small and calm, with live "answers in: 11/14" — material to shout over the heartbeat ("three teams still thinking!").

**Why it becomes memorable:** Because the audience *is* the countdown. Within weeks, rooms chant the numbers with the thumps — a ritual venues can put in their marketing. "I've never seen anything like the countdown" happens precisely because nothing else counts down with the audience's own hands.

## MOMENT 4 — ANSWER REVEAL
### "The Crack"

**Emotional objective:** Convert held tension into a clean, communal payoff — the night's most repeated applause trigger, sixty times without fatigue.

**The sequence:**

1. **The silence (host-owned).** After the Close, the stage holds: question and options standing, sealed, still. No music. The host performs into this silence — the software's gift to them is that *nothing on screen competes*. This hold is the drama; it can be two seconds or twenty.
2. **The crack (host trigger).** One percussive hit. The correct option **ignites green** — full intensity against the dark stage, a single light-sweep flaring the room green for a breath — while every wrong option falls to near-black in the same instant. One survivor, bathed in light. Nothing needs reading; the room reacts on colour alone (the one-second rule, at room scale).
3. **The receipt (2–3s later).** A single Support-scale line lands beneath: **"9 of 14 teams got it."** This little stat is the applause engine — it tells the room whether to cheer ("we're in the nine!") or groan together, and it gives the host instant material ("FIVE teams didn't know that?!").

**Handsets:** Dark in Eyes Up through the hold; each phone's private green/red verdict lands a half-beat *after* the stage cracks (Handset B9/B10) — the roar and the pocket-verdict arriving together, never before.

**Audio:** Silence → one hit with the crack → a short green shimmer tail → back to quiet for the host. For answers with a story, the host's voice is the explanation; the screen never paragraphs (explanations live on the host's dashboard, not the stage).

**Pacing:** Crack to receipt: three seconds. Receipt to next deal: whenever the host says. The reveal itself is nearly instantaneous — satisfaction comes from the *snap* of resolution after the held silence, not from a slow unveiling.

**Why it becomes memorable:** The room turning green. Sixty reveals a night, and the colour physics never gets old — because between reveals, the stage spends green *nowhere else*.

## MOMENT 5 — LEADERBOARD REVEAL
### "The Overtake"

**Emotional objective:** Make standings feel like a race broadcast, not a results table — drama for the top, dignity for the bottom, a story for the middle — at pace.

**What the audience sees:** The stage clears; **"STANDINGS"** stamps in. Rows build **from the bottom, fast** — the lower half arrives in quick, kind rhythm (no dwelling on last place; their crests arrive with exactly the same polish as everyone's). As the build climbs, it slows. From fifth place, rows land one per beat. Then the reveal *stops* below the top three — hold — and the podium places land one at a time, third, second, a longer breath, first, each with a rising note.

**The overtake is the star:** when standings have changed, teams don't appear in new positions — they **move**. A climbing crest travels visibly upward past others, purple glow-trail behind it, with a broadcast-style caption: **"▲ THE QUIZZLY BEARS — up 3."** The Display calls out at most *two* movers per leaderboard (the biggest climber, plus one the host selects) — the restraint keeps it a story, not a spreadsheet of arrows. Falling teams simply settle lower, uncaptioned, unmocked.

**Handsets:** Eyes Up through the build; each team's phone strikes its private Hit + rank card the instant their row lands on stage (Handset B17's rolling wave, in miniature) — a ripple of buzzes climbing the room in sync with the screen.

**Audio:** A propulsive but short build bed (broadcast-sting energy, not lounge music), rising cue per top-three row, one clean hit on first place, out. Total telemetry: a full leaderboard plays in well under a minute — drama must never cost pace (the Bible's law: spectacle yields to tempo).

**Host experience:** The dashboard shows the host the *coming* order before the room sees it, flagged with the movers and the gaps — so the host narrates like a sports commentator who has seen the replay: "someone in this room has jumped three places…"

**Why it becomes memorable:** Mid-table teams hear their name in lights for *movement*, not just position — the overtake caption is a memory a 6th-place team takes home. And the top-three hold teaches the room a reflex: when the build pauses, cheer.

## MOMENT 6 — SPIN TO WIN
### "The Machine"

**Emotional objective:** Take one team from anonymity to spotlight in ninety seconds, and make luck feel glamorous. This is the night's champagne moment — glittering, quick, pure pleasure.

**The sequence:**

1. **The summons (~10s).** The stage floods deep purple; **SPIN TO WIN** ignites in Bruno Ace SC — one of its two on-stage set-piece appearances — with its own signature audio (bright, metallic, arena-glam; the mood shifts the moment it sounds). Then the earning is honoured: **"Fastest correct answer: [crest] [TEAM NAME]"** stamps on. This matters — the room must know the spotlight was *won by speed*, not raffled. Applause point one.
2. **The handover (host-owned).** The slot machine builds itself on stage — a colossal, jewel-lit, physical-feeling machine, reels shut. Caption: **"[Team] is at the wheel."** The chosen team's phones transform into the SPIN control (Handset B14); every other phone drops to Eyes Up with the caption. The host works the table: "Right — who's pressing?"
3. **The charge.** As the team holds their SPIN control, the machine on stage *audibly and visibly charges* — reels trembling, light climbing, a rising whine — the entire room watching a machine respond live to one person's thumb. This is the controller doctrine made theatre: their hand, our stage.
4. **The spin (5–8s).** Release. Reels explode into motion with full mechanical glamour — blur, clatter, flying light. Then the choreography of suspense: reels land **one at a time**, left to right, each with a heavy mechanical *chunk* every phone in the room strikes in sync. First reel — reaction. Second reel — the room does the maths, noise rising. Beat before the third… 
5. **The verdict.** The last reel lands honestly (see below). A win detonates a Gear-2 celebration — the machine erupts in light, the prize stamps at Statement scale, winning phones Cascade. A miss lands with grace: a warm brass shrug of a sting, caption "So close, [team]" — and the host is pre-fed the consolation line.

**A challenge on suspense mechanics:** slot machines run on engineered near-misses, and it's tempting to fake them. **We never fake outcomes.** The spin resolves whatever the game logic honestly produced; our drama comes from *reel-by-reel pacing*, not manufactured heartbreak. A show that plays fair for one team a night keeps the trust of all fourteen — and trust is a premium feature.

**Why it becomes memorable:** One thumb controlling a giant machine while sixty phones clunk with the reels. The chosen table films it; the rest of the room films *them*. It is the night's most photographed ninety seconds before the finale.

## MOMENT 7 — THE HARD DECK
### "The Crucible"

**Emotional objective:** The signature drama. One team, escalating stakes, and one public decision that reveals character. This must be the moment people name the next day: *"…and then the Hard Deck happened."*

**Act I — The wheel (selection).** The stage cuts to near-black. **THE HARD DECK** burns on in Bruno Ace SC with its signature audio — darker than Spin to Win's, low strings and pulse; regulars' stomachs should drop pleasantly at the first note. The selection wheel builds: every team's crest set into a great dark drum. It spins on the host's trigger — fast, then the long deceleration, crests ticking past — and here the room-instrument plays its best note: **every phone in the venue Pulses in time with the slowing ticks.** Sixty people feeling the wheel slow in their hands. It stops. One crest ignites. That table erupts; every other table exhales — *both* reactions are the entertainment.

**Act II — The walk-up.** The chosen crest sweeps to centre stage, enthroned, their name at Statement scale. The host invites the team up — or to stand at their table (the host chooses per venue and per team; software supports both, pressures neither — never force theatre on the shy). Their phones ignite into the Hard Deck controller (Handset B15); all other phones sit in Eyes Up, pot-mirrored. The stakes ladder — **5 · 10 · 20 · 40** — builds up the side of the stage, bottom rung lit. The base card deals in huge, face up, with the one-time swap made visibly available. The rules need no on-screen paragraphs: ladder, card, two words — HIGHER, LOWER. The format explains itself in one look.

**Act III — The climb.** Each guess is a ritual: the team's choice flashes on stage the instant they commit (their thumb → the theatre), a breath of stillness — then the next card **flips with a heavy strike that every phone in the room feels.** Right: the card ignites, the pot counts up, the next ladder rung lights, a rising bed of tension underneath the whole climb, one notch tighter per rung. Wrong or tie: the light collapses out of the ladder — one dignified falling note, the room's collective *ooof*, and a caption that honours the run before the host takes over.

**Act IV — Stick or Gamble.** The stage transforms into the decision: the banked pot at Statement scale, flanked by the two futures — **STICK** (calm gold-edged glow) and **GAMBLE** (purple fire, showing the next rung's worth). The music drops to a single held pulse. And this is the genius the handset gives us: their commit is **hold-to-charge**, and the stage mirrors the charging in real time. The room *watches the team's hesitation as light* — the bar filling toward GAMBLE, faltering, releasing, the roar — "GAMBLE! GAMBLE!" — no screen has ever shown a decision being *almost made* before. This is Quiz-It's Millionaire pause, made visible.

**Act V — The verdict.** Final card. The longest hold in the format — the host stretches it as far as they dare — flip, strike, and either the pot **detonates upward into their score** (Gear-3 celebration, their phones alight, caption: "Banked. [Team] +40") or collapses magnificently (dignified, brief, and honoured: **"Gambled at 40. Respect."** stamps on stage — bravery is publicly credited, because the show needs next week's teams to gamble too).

**Host experience throughout:** the dashboard is pure director's console — each act one big trigger, the odds-relevant facts (cards remaining high/low) privately visible so the host can stoke the room like a poker commentator.

**Why it becomes memorable:** It's the only moment in the night where the room watches a *decision* rather than an answer. Knowledge is private; nerve is public. Strangers chant at a table they've never met. That story gets told at work on Monday — with the team's name in it.

## MOMENT 8 — ROUND FINISH
### "The Act Break"

**Emotional objective:** Close a chapter with a snap, pay a small emotional bill, and reset the room's social energy without losing the night's momentum.

**What the audience sees:** The final reveal of the round settles; then **"END OF ROUND 2"** stamps across the stage with a single confident sweep — the same ceremonial stamp language as the Close, one gear warmer. Beneath it, the round's story in three fast broadcast captions, dealt one per beat: the round's fastest team, the biggest climber, and one host-picked stat ("Only one team knew Q7…"). Then the stage exhales into the **intermission canvas**: ambient bloom, the standings resting quietly to one side, the wordmark breathing — a broadcast holding-shot the room can glance at all break. This canvas is also the venue's earned slot: a tastefully framed house message ("Kitchen closes at 10") in the show's own typography — never an ad that breaks the world, always the show lending the venue its voice.

**Handsets:** The private round receipt (Handset B16) lands as the stamp hits — public chapter-close and personal scorecard in the same breath — then phones rest in idle. Nothing demands attention during the break; the product's confidence shows in its willingness to go quiet.

**Audio:** One warm stamp-and-sweep sting, the three caption ticks, then *out* — venue's own music or the host's patter owns the break. 

**Host experience:** The dashboard flips to the interval state: next round pre-loaded and visible, a "resume" trigger armed, the three captions editable/skippable — the host decides how much ceremony each act break deserves.

**Why it becomes memorable:** Not as a highlight but as *structure* — act breaks are what make the night feel like a produced show with chapters instead of a long list of questions. The three-caption story gives every break one name-check nobody expected.

## MOMENT 9 — PODIUM REVEAL
### "The Ascent"

**Emotional objective:** Make three teams feel like medalists and the whole room feel the night mattering. Every reveal earned, every pause deliberate. This is Gear 4's opening movement.

**The sequence:**

1. **The gathering (host-triggered).** The stage empties completely — slow fade to the deepest black of the night, the room dimming with it. The heartbeat returns, slower than ever. One line, Whisper-scale, alone: **"The final standings."** Total audio silence except the heartbeat. The host's voice owns the dark: "Three teams are about to step onto the podium…" Every phone in the room: Eyes Up, still, silent. The room has never been this quiet.
2. **Third place.** A pillar of light rises from the stage floor — bronze-edged — and the third-place crest and name ascend onto it with a rising orchestral note and a single room-wide phone-strike. Hold for their applause; the host salutes them. *(Their phone alone breaks Eyes Up: rank card, warm Hit.)*
3. **Second place.** The taller silver pillar. Same ritual — but before the name lands, a longer hold in darkness, because everyone in the room is doing the same maths: *if it's not us in second… we might have won.* This pause is the maximum-tension frame of the entire night (the Bible called it; the show delivers it). Name lands. One table's mixed roar — thrilled and gutted at once — is a sound the whole room enjoys.
4. **The gap.** Beat. Heartbeat only. The centre pillar begins to rise — gold-edged, taller than both — *empty*. The host has the night's best line teed up, and the software holds the empty gold pillar for as long as the host dares.

The podium does not finish here. First place is not a reveal. It is the next moment entire.

**Why it becomes memorable:** Reverse order is old; the *empty gold pillar* is ours. An unnamed throne rising in silence while a whole room stares at it — that image is the poster of the night.

## MOMENT 10 — CHAMPION CELEBRATION
### "The Coronation"

**Emotional objective:** The defining moment of Quiz-It — the one filmed vertically on a hundred phones, the reason venues advertise the night by name. One table's greatest Thursday ever; everyone else's proof that this show is worth winning.

**The detonation.** On the host's final trigger: the winning crest and name **slam onto the gold pillar at full Statement scale** as the entire Display — and therefore the entire room — floods champagne gold for the only time all night. Confetti storms the stage (screen-confetti dense enough to read as weather, brief enough to honour the battery-and-restraint law — it storms, then it drifts, then it's done). The sonic logo detonates into the winners' **victory song** — their moment scored like a title win. The winning table's phones erupt in gold with the night's longest Cascade (Handset B18); every other phone rests dark one beat longer, then lights gently with its own generous curtain call. Around the room: one table on its feet, and — because the light physically changed the whole venue — everyone else instinctively raising a phone to film. **Design for that vertical video:** the champion frame (crest, name, gold, confetti) is composed to sit dead-centre in a portrait crop, legible and gorgeous in a nine-second story clip. That clip is Quiz-It's best advertisement, and it is filmed by customers, free, weekly.

**The photo ritual (~60–90s in).** As the song rides, the stage transforms into the **champion's backdrop**: the gold frame opens to hold a live camera moment — winning team called up by the host, arranged in front of the Display, which now displays their name, crest, the date and venue, and the wordmark around a framed void designed to have people standing in it. The venue photographs them *against their own victory graphic*; the same composition becomes the team's share card (Handset B19). The screen literally makes the photo — no banner, no printed backdrop, no setup. This is a venue-selling feature disguised as a celebration.

**The timing of generosity:** the full coronation — detonation, song peak, photo — runs two to three minutes, all host-paced. Then, deliberately, the show *bows out*: gold fades to the warm ambient stage, standings resting, the wordmark breathing, one final caption — **"QUIZ-IT · see you next week"** with the venue's next date. The music hands back to the venue. The host thanks the room over the show's quietest, most confident state. Meanwhile every phone in the house is showing "Your Night" — private stats, superlatives, share cards — so the show's last act happens in sixty palms at once.

**A challenge on the ending:** the instinct is to end at maximum volume. Wrong. The unforgettable ending is *detonation, then grace* — the show that roars and then bows leaves the room feeling the evening was hosted, not processed. The last thing people see is not fireworks; it's an invitation back.

**Why it becomes memorable:** Gold that appears once a night, on a screen that just spent three hours earning it. A photo the winners couldn't have staged. A hundred phone-lights raised in a purple-then-gold room. And a show classy enough to end quietly. That combination is the brand.

# PART C — THE NIGHT AS ONE PIECE

Played end to end, the ten moments compose the show the Bible promised:

**Ignition** (the room becomes an audience) → **the Deal**, sixty times, the ritual heartbeat → **the Close**, the countdown the room performs → **the Crack**, the room turning green → **the Overtake**, the race story → **the Machine**, luck made glamorous → **the Crucible**, nerve made public → **the Act Breaks**, chapters that breathe → **the Ascent**, the empty gold pillar → **the Coronation**, and the bow.

One rule binds them all: **the screen loads the moment; the host fires it; the room completes it.** Any future animation, celebration, or feature that cannot find its place in that sentence does not belong on the Display.
