# QUIZ-IT BRAND VOICE GUIDE

**The writing guide for every word Quiz-It ever displays**
Quiz-It · Powered by Mac Entertainment · by Sonya Mac
Version 1.0 · July 2026 · Brand Director's specification, governed by all locked references

---

# 1. THE VOICE

Quiz-It writes like **a great host's favourite floor manager**: someone who has run a thousand shows, wastes no words, loves the audience, and never panics. Three traits, in tension by design:

- **Confident** — declarative, present tense, no hedging. The show never says "please wait," "oops," or "something went wrong." It says what *is*: "Answers are in." "Your host will be right back."
- **Warm** — the reader is a guest, never a user. Second person, always on their side, especially in defeat.
- **Brief** — the one-second rule applies to words. If a line can lose a word and keep its meaning, it loses the word. If a sentence needs a comma, it's usually two lines or one shorter line.

**The universal test:** read any line aloud in a busy bar. If it sounds like a broadcast caption or something a good host would say — it passes. If it sounds like software, a form, or a terms-and-conditions page — rewrite.

## Hard rules

Never: exclamation marks in system text (excitement is the light and sound's job — a calm line under a detonating screen is *more* confident); emoji 🔒; jargon ("submit," "input," "session," "error," "loading," "invalid"); apologies from the machine; ALL-CAPS beyond three-word ceremonial stamps; corporate padding ("successfully," "in order to," "at this time").

Always: contractions ("you're," "it's" — the voice is spoken, not written); verbs over nouns ("Join the show," not "Team registration"); the show's vocabulary ("the show," "the stage," "the room," "your host" — never "the app," "the system," "the screen," "the platform"). All copy must suit a mixed international UAE audience 🔒 — no innuendo, no alcohol references in system text (the *host* makes the drinks call, the software never does), no cultural references that don't travel.

# 2. THREE SURFACES, ONE VOICE, THREE VOLUMES

The voice never changes; its **volume and intimacy** do:

- **Display — the announcer.** Speaks to the whole room, in headlines. Short, declarative, ceremonial. It announces; it never chats, explains, or addresses individuals except to name them in glory ("▲ THE QUIZZLY BEARS — up 3").
- **Handset — the confidant.** Speaks to one team, quietly, in the second person. It can be wry, personal, encouraging. This is where the brand's warmth lives ("That one stung — next one's yours.").
- **Host console — the colleague.** Speaks to a professional mid-performance. Zero ceremony, maximum utility, everything phrased as material to say aloud or a decision to make ("Fastest: Quizzly Bears — promote them?"). Never cute — a joke on the console is a joke at the host's expense.

Rule of address: the Display says *names*, the Handset says *you*, the console says *facts*.

---

# 3. HUMOUR

Quiz-It is funny the way a well-run show is funny: **dry, brief, and never at the player's expense.** The software plays the straight man; the host is the comedian. System humour lives almost entirely on the Handset, in the variation pools, and follows three laws:

1. **Punch at the situation, never the player.** "Round's long. Stay in it." — never "Better luck next time, genius."
2. **One wry line per moment, maximum.** Humour is seasoning; a screen that keeps joking is a screen begging to be liked.
3. **The stakes earn the joke.** Light moments (waiting, lobby) may be playful; sacred moments (the Close, the podium, a bust) are never joked over by the system — those laughs belong to the host.

# 4. THE COPY BOOK

The canonical lines. Engineers and designers take copy from here; new lines must sound like these.

## Joining

- Entry: **"Join the show."** (never "Sign up," "Register," "Get started")
- Name prompt: **"Who are you tonight?"**
- Name taken: **"That name's taken tonight — make it yours."**
- Confirmed: **"You're in, [team]."**
- On the crest wall: **"You're on the wall. Eyes up."**
- Rejoin: **"Welcome back — rejoin as [team]?"**
- Late join: **"You're in — Round 2, Question 4 is live."** (what's now, never what's missed)

## Waiting

- Lobby: **"Waiting for your host…"** / **"14 teams in the room tonight."**
- Pre-show pulse: **"Five minutes. Settle in."** / at T-0:30: **"It's time."**
- Between rounds: silence is the default — the idle state needs no caption. If one is used: **"Round 3 coming up."**
- Host pause: **"Your host will be right back."** (the show's voice, never an error's)

## Play

- Lock bar: **"LOCK IT IN"** · Locked: **"LOCKED."** / **"Answer sealed until the reveal."**
- Changed answer, first time: **"Changing costs your speed bonus."** (honest, once, then never nags)
- Answers closed: **"Answers are in. Eyes up."**
- No answer given: **"No answer this time."** (full stop — no scolding clause)

## Countdown wording

The Close needs almost no words — the numerals are the copy. The only sanctioned text: **"TIME'S UP"** (never "Time has expired," "Round over," or a softening "Time!"). On the console: **"Closing in 5…"** The pre-show clock: **"SHOW STARTS · 08:42."** Countdown copy law: numbers do the talking; words appear only at zero.

## Congratulations

Scaled like the celebrations — the words stay calm as the stakes rise, because the *light* is doing the shouting:

- Correct: **"CORRECT."** (the full stop is the swagger)
- Points: **"+150"** / **"Fastest in the room."**
- Streak: **"Five straight. [Team] is hot."**
- Round: **"▲ Up 3 tonight — the comeback is on."**
- Hard Deck bank: **"Banked. [Team] +40."**
- Champion: **"CHAMPIONS."** — the biggest moment of the night gets one word. Adding more would shrink it.

## Encouragement & failures

The defeated-player lines are the most important copy in the product (nine tables lose every night). Variation pool, all following the pattern *acknowledge briefly → point forward*:

- **"That one stung — next one's yours."**
- **"Round's long. Stay in it."**
- **"Wrong — but fast. Speed like that wins rounds."**
- Bust (Hard Deck): **"Gambled at 40. Respect."**
- Night's end, non-winners: **"4th. One Hard Deck away. Next week."** / **"Seventh tonight. The comeback starts Thursday."**

Never: "Unlucky!", "Wrong answer," "You lost," "Nice try" (condescension in two words), or any line that dwells ("So close to the podium, that must hurt").

## Error messages — the show never breaks

The word "error" does not exist in Quiz-It. Trouble is narrated the way a stage manager narrates: calmly, with the show carrying on:

- Connection lost: **"Reconnecting to the show…"** → after ~10s: **"Still with you — the show carries on on the big screen."**
- Recovered: nothing, or **"Welcome back — Q6 is live, you're 4th."**
- Can't join (show full/ended): **"Tonight's show is underway — ask your host to wave you in."** / **"That's a wrap tonight. Same time next week."**
- Console (host-facing) drops ceremony entirely: **"Display offline — check HDMI. Show state is safe."** (What happened · what to do · what's safe. Three beats, always.)

Trouble-copy laws: never blame the player or their phone; never say "try again" without saying what will happen; never show codes or technical nouns to players (consoles may — hosts are crew).

## Announcements (Display captions)

Format: fact, then flourish — maximum eight words: **"STANDINGS"** · **"Fastest this round: THE QUIZZLY BEARS"** · **"Only one team knew Q7."** · **"Doors closing on Round 1 — join now."** · the sign-off: **"QUIZ-IT · see you next week."**

---

# 5. GRAMMAR & MECHANICS

Sentence case for handset and console text; stamps in caps ≤3 words. Full stops on statements, even short ones ("LOCKED." / "CORRECT.") — the period is the brand's punctuation, the sound of certainty. No exclamation marks, no ellipses except the reconnecting line and host-hold teases ("The answer is…"). Numbers as numerals, always ("14 teams," never "fourteen teams"). Team names always rendered exactly as entered, never truncated on the Display 🔒. En-dashes for asides, the interpunct (·) for ceremonial separators. British English throughout ("colour," "programme" for TV, "cheque" never needed — but if in doubt, UK).

**The final test for any new line:** does it sound like a show, and would you be happy hearing it read aloud by the host to a room you're sitting in? If either answer wobbles, the line isn't done.
