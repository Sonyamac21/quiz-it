# QUIZ-IT VENUE OPERATIONS MANUAL

**The official guide to staging a Quiz-It night**
Quiz-It · Powered by Mac Entertainment · by Sonya Mac
Version 1.0 · July 2026

This manual is for venue managers and hosts. It contains no design theory — only what makes the show land in a real room. The standard it protects: a guest should walk in and think *"I've never seen anything like this,"* and that impression is made as much by sightlines and speaker placement as by anything on screen.

---

# 1. VENUE SETUP

## 1.1 The room audit (do once, before your first show)

Walk the room at show time on a normal night and answer honestly: Where does everyone's eye naturally rest? Where is it loudest? Which tables can't see the main TV? Where would a host stand without blocking anything? Quiz-It nights are won in this walk. The three non-negotiables: **every playing table must see a screen, hear the host, and reach the bar without crossing the "stage."**

## 1.2 Display placement

- The main Display should be the **largest screen in the room**, placed high enough to clear standing heads (bottom edge roughly at standing eye level), centred on the largest block of tables.
- Distance rule of thumb: the screen should look "big" from the farthest playing table — if you must squint at the question text from a table, that table isn't a playing table; either add a repeater screen or don't seat teams there.
- Rooms with wings or pillars: use repeater screens mirroring the Display. Every repeater shows the identical picture — never a different feed, never sport on one and quiz on the other in the same playing area. Split attention kills the shared-moment effect the show is built on.
- Kill or dim competing screens in the playing area during show time. One live sport screen behind the host will beat the best quiz question ever written.
- Avoid placing the Display where daylight or bar spotlights hit it. The show is dark-stage by design; glare is its natural enemy.

## 1.3 Audio setup

- Feed the show's audio through the **house PA**, not the TV's speakers. TV speakers make the biggest moments sound small, and the countdown heartbeat needs low end a flatscreen cannot make.
- Set levels during a test: the tension bed should sit *under* conversation; the countdown and reveals should cut *through* it; the coronation should be the loudest thing of the night without distorting. If in doubt, calibrate to the countdown — when the heartbeat plays, people should feel it, and still hear the host over it.
- Bluetooth audio paths can add delay and drop under load; use a wired feed from the show machine to the PA wherever possible, and test the full path — including any HDMI audio — before doors, every show night.
- The show is designed to survive silence: if audio fails mid-show, keep playing — everything important is on screen. Fix audio at the next interval, not mid-round.

## 1.4 Microphone advice

- The host uses a mic in any room bigger than a single bar area — projection hosting for two hours kills voices and energy.
- Wireless handheld beats headset for this format: the handheld is a prop, a talking stick, and a crowd-pointing device.
- Set the mic **louder than the show audio**. The mix rule all night: voice on top, always. (The system ducks its own beds under the mic automatically; your PA levels should agree with it.)
- Have a spare (charged batteries or a wired backup on the host stand). A dead mic at Stick-or-Gamble is the most preventable disaster in the format.

## 1.5 Lighting

- The show thrives in a **dim room**: the Display is the lighting rig, and its colour changes (the purple flood, the green flash, the gold coronation) visibly repaint a dim room and disappear in a bright one. Dim the playing area to "comfortable conversation" level — not darkness; people are eating.
- Keep the bar and walkways at normal brightness. The contrast helps: stepping into the playing area should feel like entering the show.
- Never point lights at the Display or the host's face from below. If the venue has any controllable colour lighting, set it warm-neutral and *static* — the screen provides the drama; house lights that chase or strobe fight it.

## 1.6 Host positioning

- The host stands **beside** the Display, never in front of it, angled to see both room and screen with a small turn. The picture the room should read: presenter and screen as one stage.
- The host console (laptop/tablet) sits on a stand at waist height at that position — glanceable without head-down hunching. A host buried in a laptop reads as a technician; the console is built for half-second glances, so stage it that way.
- Give the host a route: they should be able to walk the floor during questions and be back at the console in ten seconds. The Space-bar/clicker drive exists precisely so the host can roam.

# 2. PLAYER FLOW & JOINING

- **Table cards on every table, always** — including the tables you think won't play. The NFC tap is the premium first impression ("tap to play" and it just works); the QR is the reliable fallback; the short URL on the card is the fallback's fallback which the host can read aloud.
- Staff briefing (two minutes, every show night): every server can say what Quiz-It is in one sentence — *"Live game show, your phone's the controller, tap the purple card to play"* — and knows that joining takes under a minute, teams are per-table, and it's free to play (or whatever the venue's offer is).
- Seat walk-ins *inside* the playing area during pre-show — proximity converts. A table that can see other tables joining on the big screen joins too.
- Reserve the best-sightline tables for booked quiz teams. Regulars who book should never end up behind a pillar.
- Late arrivals: the door never closes. Staff seat them, point at the card, and the show folds them in automatically — no fuss, no announcements needed unless the host chooses.

# 3. RUNNING THE NIGHT

## 3.1 Show pacing (the venue's interest, stated plainly)

The format's natural shape — three GK rounds, Spin to Win, The Hard Deck, podium — runs comfortably in about two hours with two intervals. Protect these commercial beats:

- **Intervals are order windows.** The show goes properly quiet at act breaks by design; that silence is for your tills. Brief servers to sweep the floor the moment the "END OF ROUND" stamp appears.
- **The T-5 drinks call** is in the host's script for a reason: a room with fresh drinks at Question One stays seated through Round 1.
- Don't stretch the night. A tight two hours that ends on the coronation high beats a baggy three hours every time — people should leave wanting next week, not checking watches.
- The host controls all pacing from the console; venues should agree the target end time with the host before doors, then let the host drive.

## 3.2 Audience management

- **Noise is success.** A loud room at the reveal is the product working. The host manages the room's energy with the mic and the show's own silences — staff only ever intervene for genuine disruption, discreetly, away from the stage.
- Big groups: seat them as one team or split them into rival teams *at adjacent tables* — the rivalry is free entertainment for the whole room.
- The spotlight moments (Spin to Win, The Hard Deck) sometimes land on shy tables. The format supports playing from the table — the host never forces a walk-up, and staff shouldn't either.
- Disputes ("we typed it faster!") go to the host, who has score-adjust powers and a team-history drawer for exactly this. Staff route disputes to the host; nobody argues scoring at the bar.
- Phones filming the finale are not a problem to manage — they're the point. Make sure the room lights aren't so bright they flatten the gold.

## 3.3 Best practices from winning venues

Same night, same time, every week — ritual builds the crowd; a movable quiz night never compounds. Name the night and market it as **Quiz-It** (the brand pulls better than "quiz night"). Photograph the podium and champion-backdrop moment weekly and post it — that's your ad, shot for free. Offer a winners' perk that returns (a bar tab *next week* beats cash tonight — it books the rematch). Keep a "house team" of staff on quiet weeks to guarantee atmosphere. And protect the format: no venue-side interruptions (raffles, announcements) inside rounds — park them in the intervals where the show has built you silence to use.

# 4. TROUBLESHOOTING

The show is built to degrade gracefully. The operating principle for every problem: **the show carries on; fix things at intervals.**

| Symptom | First response | If it persists |
|---|---|---|
| Display goes dark | Check HDMI at both ends; check TV input hasn't switched | Move show to the repeater screen; host announces "eyes to the side screen" and plays on |
| No show audio | Check PA feed and volume path from the show machine | Play on silent — the show is fully legible muted; fix at interval |
| Host console frozen | Refresh the console — the show state recovers automatically, including the running timer | Swap to backup device (any browser, log back in); the show resumes where it was |
| A team's phone won't join | Try QR instead of NFC; try the short URL; check they're on venue Wi-Fi or mobile data | Host waves them in verbally and adds the team from the console |
| A team drops mid-question | Nothing — their phone shows "Reconnecting" and recovers itself; their locked answer is safe | "Rejoin as [team]?" flow on any phone restores identity and score |
| Venue Wi-Fi struggling | Players switch to mobile data (the handset is lightweight by design) | Prioritise the show machine on wired/strongest connection; players on 4G/5G |
| Wrong score / dispute | Host opens the team drawer, adjusts with a reason, moves on — under ten seconds | Log it for post-show; never relitigate live |
| Fire alarm / genuine interruption | Host hits Pause ("Your host will be right back"), house lights up | Resume from the exact state when clear — the show holds its place |

**The golden rule of trouble:** the room takes its cue from the host. The console never panics on screen, the host never panics on mic, and 90% of the room will never know anything happened.

# 5. THE SHOW-NIGHT CHECKLIST

**Before doors:** Display on the right input, idle loop running · PA fed and levels tested with the countdown cue · mic tested, spare ready · console charged, on stand, logged in · table cards on every table · competing screens off/dimmed in the playing area · lights set dim-warm · staff briefed (one-sentence pitch + interval sweep) · end time agreed with host.

**At intervals:** server sweep · glance at Display and audio · winners' perk stocked for the finale.

**After the show:** post the champion photo · note the night's issues while fresh · confirm next week's slot on the way out — to the winning table first.
