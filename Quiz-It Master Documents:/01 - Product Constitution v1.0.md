# QUIZ-IT PRODUCT NON-NEGOTIABLES

**The Product Constitution**
Quiz-It · Powered by Mac Entertainment · by Sonya Mac
Version 1.0 · July 2026

Read this before changing anything. These rules distil every Quiz-It specification into permanent law. They are not guidelines; a change that violates one is wrong even if it tests well, ships fast, or looks beautiful. Amendments require the same deliberation that created them.

---

## I. IDENTITY

1. Quiz-It is a show that contains a quiz — never quiz software.
2. The product turns a room of strangers into an audience. Every feature is judged by whether it serves that.
3. The Display is the theatre. The Host is mission control. The Handset is the controller. These roles never blur.
4. The screen loads the moment; the host fires it; the room completes it. Nothing auto-advances a show beat.
5. The software exists to make the host look brilliant. It supports the performer; it never competes with them.
6. "Premium" means broadcast confidence — F1 graphics, not boutique luxury. Legibility beats elegance in every conflict.
7. Quiz-It never feels like a website: no links, no forms, no breadcrumbs, no browser-default anything in the show.
8. The wordmark is "QUIZ-" in #BE26C1 and "IT" in white. The tagline is "Quiz-It · Powered by Mac Entertainment · by Sonya Mac."
9. Locked names never change and retired names never return: The Hard Deck, Danger Zone, Wipeout Mode, Multi Tap, Spin to Win.
10. All content — questions, copy, visuals, sponsor cards — must suit a mixed international UAE audience. No exceptions, no edgy moments.

## II. THE ONE-SECOND WORLD

11. Every screen communicates its single most important fact to a distracted person within one second.
12. One focal point per screen. Two competing elements means the screen is wrong.
13. Information is prioritised by emotion, not data: what the room cares about gets the size.
14. The state of the show is always legible without interaction, on every surface.
15. Nothing ever blocks the host's next action — no animation, transition, or sound may make the room wait.
16. The Display never shows software machinery: no dialogs, tables, spinners, settings, or states-of-the-system.

## III. COLOUR

17. The stage is dark: near-black violet (#0A0118 family). Never pure black, never white backgrounds, never grey neutrals.
18. Purple #BE26C1 is the brand and everything that is "Quiz-It itself."
19. Green means correct and nothing else, ever.
20. Red means incorrect (and Reverse) and nothing else, ever — never warnings, timers, errors, or emphasis.
21. Yellow means Boost only. Blue means Time-Out only.
22. Gold means victory and appears only at the podium, coronation, and winners' artefacts. Silver and bronze exist only beside it.
23. There is no warning colour and no error red anywhere in the product. Trouble speaks in purple, dimness, and calm words.
24. Focus is created by darkening everything else, never by making the focal element louder.
25. No colour may carry meaning alone: every meaningful state is also coded in brightness, words, or behaviour (monochrome test mandatory).

## IV. TYPE & WORDS

26. Bruno Ace SC appears in exactly three places: the wordmark, Spin to Win titles, The Hard Deck titles. Inter does everything else.
27. Three sizes of intent per surface — Statement, Support, Whisper. A fourth size is a design failure.
28. Changing numbers always use tabular figures. A number that cuts to a new value instead of animating is a spreadsheet.
29. Questions never exceed three lines on the Display. Rewrite, never shrink.
30. No italics. ALL-CAPS only for ceremonial stamps of three words or fewer. No ellipsis truncation on the Display.
31. The product speaks like a great floor manager: confident, warm, brief. No exclamation marks in system text, no emoji, no jargon, no apologies from the machine.
32. The words "error," "submit," "loading," "invalid," "user," "app," and "system" never face a player.
33. Defeat copy acknowledges briefly and points forward. The product never mocks, never dwells, never says "you lost."
34. "CHAMPIONS." — the biggest moments get the fewest words.

## V. MOTION

35. Motion is punctuation: it marks state changes only. Motion during a state is a defect (exceptions: the breathing bloom, the draining timer ring).
36. Everything arrives fast with one small settle and leaves faster. Nothing drifts, floats, morphs, or bounces twice.
37. Linear movement is banned except hold-to-commit charge fills, which are linear because honesty reads linear.
38. Nothing animates longer than 1.2 seconds. Longer drama is built from stillness between animations, never slower animation.
39. Maximum two simultaneous animations per surface, one subordinate.
40. Stillness is a first-class element: every animation ends in a composed frame, because the frame is seen longer than the motion.
41. Screen-shake is banned. Camera-style zooms and pans are banned. Parallax is banned.
42. Falling teams are never animated downward, captioned, or mocked. The show celebrates rising; it never stages falling.
43. Celebrations are rationed by gear (play → event → set-piece → finale) and no moment may borrow from a higher gear. Confetti exists only at the coronation.
44. Every celebration peaks fast, ends completely, and leaves the room to the host.

## VI. SOUND & SILENCE

45. The host is the soundtrack. Nothing plays over the microphone uninvited; beds duck within 100ms of the host speaking.
46. Audio marks changes of state, never states. A sound that plays continuously is wallpaper and gets cut.
47. The show is 100% playable muted. Audio is amplification, never information.
48. Silence is engineered and sacred: the reveal hold, the post-countdown seal, the empty gold pillar, the bow. Nothing may sound during a host hold.
49. Every sound heard ten or more times a night ships as a variation pool, never a single take.
50. The heartbeat (deep dual-thump) is the sonic signature. The sonic logo plays twice a night — Ignition and Coronation — and stays sacred by rarity.
51. There is no voiceover. The human has the voice.
52. The handset is silent by design. Haptics are the player's private channel, and no haptic is ever the sole carrier of information.
53. Losses receive one dignified low sound, never a buzzer, klaxon, or comedy sting from the system.

## VII. THE HANDSET

54. The handset is a game controller, never a form. Everything in timed play fits on one screen with no scrolling.
55. Every tappable element during play lives in the bottom 40% of the screen, sized for a confident thumb holding a drink.
56. Answering is two-stage: tap to choose, lock to commit. The Lock is the product's signature physical sensation.
57. Locked is purple, not green. Correctness is revealed on the show's schedule; the phone never spoils the stage.
58. The phone tells your story; the Display tells the room's. The handset never duplicates the big screen — it personalises it.
59. Eyes Up is law: during shared show moments the handset goes dark and lifts heads. The phone taps the player when it needs them, which is what lets them ignore it safely.
60. Irreversible acts (spending cards, Stick or Gamble) are hold-to-commit. No team loses anything to a slipped thumb.
61. The handset's leaderboard is you-centric: your rank plus the gap above and below. Full standings belong to the Display.
62. Idle states are near-black and still. A phone at 40% battery must finish the show.
63. Connection trouble is whispered, never shouted: dim, one calm line, the show visibly carrying on. Rejoining always feels like being waved back to your seat.
64. Joining takes under a minute, one welcome regardless of door (NFC, QR, URL), and the door never closes — late teams are folded in without apology or fuss.

## VIII. THE DISPLAY

65. The Display is never blank and never busy. Between moments it breathes; during moments it commits totally.
66. The Display is the venue's lighting rig: every major moment is designed as light thrown onto faces, not just pixels.
67. Reveals resolve by snap after silence — options collapse, one survivor ignites. The gap between close and reveal belongs to the host, always.
68. Leaderboards build bottom-up, fast and kind at the bottom, slowing to the top, holding below the top three. The overtake travels; at most two movers are celebrated per board.
69. First place on the podium is not a reveal — it is the empty gold pillar, then the Coronation. The pause is the product.
70. Bravery is publicly honoured: a Hard Deck bust earns "Gambled at 40. Respect." on stage.
71. The champion frame is composed for a vertical phone crop, and the finale becomes the winners' photo backdrop. The show makes the photograph.
72. The show ends with detonation, then grace: gold, song, photo — then a quiet bow and next week's date. The last frame is an invitation, not fireworks.
73. Sponsor and venue messages ride the pre-show idle loop only, in the show's own voice, and vanish from T-10. The show never wears someone else's clothes.

## IX. THE HOST CONSOLE

74. The console is calm authority: it never celebrates, never dazzles, never performs. The host must be the one person immune to the show's spectacle.
75. Fixed geography forever: ON AIR mirror, the desk, the big button. Regions never move mid-show.
76. One big button, always labelled with the show consequence ("FLIP IT," "CROWN THEM"), never the software action.
77. Everything on the console is written to be read aloud mid-sentence. Its true output is the host's next line.
78. The host always sees the answer, the incoming locks, and the coming reveal before the room. Foreknowledge is what makes performance possible.
79. Set-pieces run one-trigger-per-act with commentary fuel on tap. Destructive actions are hold-to-commit and have no keyboard shortcuts.
80. The finale is a pre-flight checklist: song, tie check, and photo decision are made before the first pillar rises.

## X. FAIRNESS, DIGNITY & THE ROOM

81. The show never lies: no fake near-misses, no manufactured outcomes, no dishonest progress. Drama comes from pacing, never deception.
82. Nine tables lose every night, and they decide whether the venue rebooks. Near-misses, superlatives, and generous curtain calls are core product, not polish.
83. Superlatives, not badges: night-scoped titles in the show's voice. No XP, no trophy cabinets, no childish gamification.
84. The spotlight is never forced: set-piece teams may play from their table. The show pressures nobody.
85. Every player gets the same show: playable silent, legible in monochrome, navigable by screen reader where the clock allows, calm under reduce-motion — with the player's dignity identical in every mode.
86. No element flashes more than three times per second, anywhere, ever.
87. Time pressure is designed fair: the clock never starts before the question lands, the host can always extend, and nothing outside answering ever has a timeout.
88. Scores are the host's to adjust, on the record, in seconds. Disputes are resolved by a human with good tools, never by "the system says."

## XI. THE FINAL LAWS

89. Good works. Excellent impresses. World-class gets applause. Every screen aims for the third.
90. If everything is special, nothing is. Rarity is the engine of every signature moment — spend gold, confetti, silence, and the sonic logo like the last of their kind.
91. When two rules conflict, the earlier section wins. When a proposal fits no rule, the answer is the Bible's: dark stage, white type, purple light — and don't ship until it feels like a show.

*End of the Quiz-It Product Constitution v1.0.*
